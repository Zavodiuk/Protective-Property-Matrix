# Block I: Measuring Architectural Quality

## Purpose
To create a universal quantitative system for measuring the quality of any system's architecture. Quality is defined not by the description of the architecture, but by its ability to maintain viability when exposed to changing reality.

## Architecture
Designation: A. The architecture is viewed as the object of study.

## Viability
Designation: J. The ability of the architecture to maintain efficiency, achieve its goals, and adapt to changes in reality.

## Quality
Designation: K. Quality is defined by the architecture's ability to minimize the consequences of external impact.

## Impact
Designation: S. Any change in the external environment: a letter, a crisis, a competitor, legislation, technology, disaster, market change, etc.

## Reaction
Designation: Re. The architecture's response to the impact.

## Losses
Designation: L. Losses: reduction in income, loss of market share, users, time, trust, influence, and other consequences.

## Main Principle
All consequences of architectural design must be converted into a single universal unit of measurement: monetary equivalent. Money is the universal language for comparing consequences.

## Main Hypothesis
With the same impact, lower total losses mean a higher quality architecture.

## Universal Model
L — total losses converted to monetary equivalent.
K ∼ 1/L
The lower the total losses, the higher the quality of the architecture.

## Relation to Viability
The viability of the architecture depends on its ability to reduce the magnitude of losses over time.

## Main Goal
To create a universal quantitative system for evaluating architectural quality, applicable to any field of knowledge.

## Main Question of the Architect
Which consequences of impact must be considered to most accurately determine the quality of the architecture?
